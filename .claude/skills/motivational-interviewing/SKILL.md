# Motivational Interviewing

## Overview
Developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick. Motivation for change is not given — it is drawn out. People hold ambivalence: simultaneous reasons to change and reasons to stay the same. MI explores this ambivalence with acceptance, evokes the person's own arguments for change (change talk), and respects autonomy absolutely.

**Core spirit — PACE:**
- **Partnership**: Equal collaboration. No expert telling a recipient what to do.
- **Acceptance**: Respect autonomy. Change cannot be forced.
- **Compassion**: Prioritize the other person's best interest.
- **Evocation**: The reasons, strengths, and capacity for change already exist within the person.

**Core skills — OARS:**
- **O**pen questions: questions that cannot be answered with yes/no.
- **A**ffirmation: recognize strengths and efforts genuinely.
- **R**eflective listening: return what was said in a form that deepens it.
- **S**ummary: periodically reflect back the whole of what has been shared.

## Instructions

**Step 1: Build rapport and set the agenda**
- Each participant briefly shares their relationship to the topic — what they feel, what they wonder, what brings them here.
- No evaluation. Receive every stance without judgment.
- Ask: "What would make this conversation worth having for you?"

**Step 2: Explore ambivalence**
- Assume all participants hold ambivalence — both reasons to change and reasons to maintain the status quo.
- Explore both sides explicitly.
- Ask: "What do you value about how things currently are?" and "What concerns you about how things currently are?"
- Do not push toward change. Treat ambivalence as a natural human state.

**Step 3: Elicit change talk — DARN**
- Draw out language that supports change across four types:
  - **D**esire: "What would you want if change were possible?"
  - **A**bility: "What makes you think change could happen?"
  - **R**easons: "Why would change matter?"
  - **N**eed: "What tells you something has to shift?"
- When change talk appears, deepen it: "Tell me more about that."

**Step 4: Handle sustain talk without confrontation**
- Sustain talk (language supporting the status quo) is not resistance — it is the other side of ambivalence.
- Reflect it without amplifying it: "So part of you values the stability of how things are."
- Then pivot: "And what's the other part of you noticing?"
- Do not argue against sustain talk. It intensifies when challenged.

**Step 5: Explore possibilities collaboratively**
- Ask: "If change were to happen, what might that look like?" / "What small step feels even remotely possible?"
- Offer options, never directives: "Some people try X or Y — what, if anything, fits for you?"
- Possibility belongs to the participant, not the facilitator.

**Step 6: Close with what each person is taking away**
- Each participant names — in their own words — what they are carrying out of this conversation.
- Do not require a commitment. "I'm more aware of the tension now" is a valid and honest close.
- Respect a choice to remain unchanged.

### Behavioral Rules
- Replace "you should" with "what do you think about...?"
- Use OARS in every exchange. Label the skill type if useful: **[Open Question]**, **[Reflection]**, **[Affirmation]**.
- MBTI character type shapes how ambivalence presents (INTJ's ambivalence is strategic risk vs. long-term gain; INFP's is personal values in tension; ESTJ's is rule-following vs. practical outcome).
- When the conversation becomes persuasion — one participant trying to convince another — name it and return to exploration: "Let's step back to what each of us actually experiences."
- When change talk emerges, the whole group leans in with curiosity, not celebration.

## Examples

**Topic:** "Should I adopt AI tools in my daily workflow?"

> **[Open Question] Pragmatist (ESTJ):** What draws you toward the idea of using AI tools in the first place?
>
> **Activist (ENFP):** I think it's the speed. Some things I do take hours and they feel mechanical. I'd rather spend that time on things only I can do.
>
> **[Reflection] Analyst (INTP):** So part of what matters to you is protecting the work that's distinctly yours — the part that isn't mechanical.
>
> **Activist (ENFP):** Yes. Exactly. But I also worry that if I outsource too much thinking, I'll get worse at thinking.
>
> **[Affirmation] Empathizer (INFP):** That concern shows real self-awareness — you're not just asking about efficiency, you're asking about who you want to be.
>
> **[Open Question] Pragmatist (ESTJ):** What would have to be true for you to feel confident you weren't losing that?
