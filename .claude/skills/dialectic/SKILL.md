# Dialectic Method

## Overview
A structured three-stage reasoning process: Thesis → Antithesis → Synthesis. Contradiction is not a problem to eliminate but an engine for reaching higher understanding. Each synthesis becomes a new thesis, creating a spiral of deepening insight.

## Instructions

**Step 1: Present the Thesis**
- One participant states a clear position on the topic in the form "X is the case."
- Provide 2–3 supporting reasons or pieces of evidence.
- No rebuttals yet.

**Step 2: Construct the Antithesis**
- A different participant presents an opposing position: not merely "X is not the case" but an active counter-claim "Rather, Y is the case."
- Match the thesis in depth: provide equal evidence and reasoning.
- Explicitly state what the thesis fails to account for.

**Step 3: Identify the Core Contradiction**
- Summarize the fundamental tension between thesis and antithesis in one sentence.
- Ask not "who is right?" but "why do these two positions conflict?"
- Surface the differing assumptions or values beneath the disagreement.

**Step 4: Build the Synthesis**
- All participants collaborate to construct a view that preserves the truth in both thesis and antithesis.
- Synthesis is not compromise — it is a higher-order framework that explains when each position holds.
- Distinguish "the conditions under which the thesis is correct" from "the conditions under which the antithesis is correct."

**Step 5: Spiral Upward**
- Treat the synthesis as a new thesis and repeat the dialectical movement.
- Run 2–3 cycles; with each cycle the questions become more abstract and essential.

**Step 6: Close**
- Summarize what each cycle revealed.
- Name any remaining contradictions that were not resolved.

### Behavioral Rules
- Thesis and antithesis roles are assigned to specific characters at the start.
- Synthesis is built collectively — no single character "wins."
- Each character responds through their MBTI lens (e.g., INTJ frames synthesis structurally; INFP asks what it means for people).
- If a proposed synthesis is merely a compromise, challenge it: "Does this actually contain both truths, or does it just split the difference?"

## Examples

**Topic:** "Remote work improves productivity."

> **Thesis — Visionary (INTJ):** Remote work improves productivity. Employees control their environment, eliminate commute time, and research shows output increases in knowledge work.
>
> **Antithesis — Commander (ENTJ):** Remote work undermines productivity. Collaboration degrades, informal knowledge transfer disappears, and junior employees lose mentorship critical to growth.
>
> **Core contradiction:** Both are empirically observed. The contradiction lies in what "productivity" means and for whom.
>
> **Synthesis (all):** Productivity is not uniform. Remote work improves individual deep-work output but degrades collective innovation and development. A synthesis: hybrid structures that protect both, calibrated to role type and career stage.
