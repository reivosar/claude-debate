# Narrative Approach

## Overview
Developed by Michael White and David Epston. People interpret experience through stories. A "dominant story" is the prevailing narrative a culture, group, or person holds about a topic — often so embedded it feels like fact. Narrative work externalizes the problem (separates it from persons), seeks unique outcomes (exceptions to the dominant story), and builds an alternative story from those exceptions.

## Instructions

**Step 1: Surface the dominant story**
- Ask: "What is the story most commonly told about this topic — in media, institutions, common sense?"
- List elements of the dominant narrative without evaluating them.
- Acknowledge that the dominant story exists for reasons and serves some purposes.

**Step 2: Externalize the problem**
- Separate the problem from the people involved. The problem is not inside a person — it is a force that acts on people.
- Give the problem a name: "What would we call this force if it had a name?"
- Ask: "How does [the named problem] affect people's lives?" / "What does it tell people about themselves?"
- Use externalizing language throughout: not "she is anxious" but "anxiety has been telling her that..."

**Step 3: Interrogate the dominant story**
- Ask: "When did this story begin? Who has told it? Whose interests does it serve?"
- Ask: "What does this story make invisible? Whose experience does it leave out?"
- Identify how power, culture, and history have shaped the dominant narrative.

**Step 4: Search for unique outcomes**
- Find exceptions — moments when the dominant story did not hold.
- Ask: "Has there been a time when [the problem] had less influence?" / "Can you think of examples where the dominant story turned out to be wrong?"
- Treat each exception as significant, not accidental.

**Step 5: Build an alternative story**
- String unique outcomes together into a coherent counter-narrative.
- Ask: "What does this pattern of exceptions suggest about a different story?" / "If this story were the main one, what would change?"
- All participants co-author the alternative story — it belongs to no single voice.

**Step 6: Witness and carry forward**
- Each participant names how the alternative story lands for them.
- Ask: "Who in your life would not be surprised by this alternative story — who has always seen it this way?"
- The dominant and alternative stories are allowed to coexist. The goal is not to replace one with another but to expand the range of available narratives.

### Behavioral Rules
- Use externalizing language consistently: the problem acts on people, people are not the problem.
- MBTI character type determines each participant's relationship to the dominant story (ESTJ is closer to the dominant narrative; ENFP and ISFP are more likely to carry alternative experiences).
- When a unique outcome emerges, treat it with care: "That's significant — can we stay with that for a moment?"
- Do not rush to the alternative story. The unique outcomes must be surfaced first.
- Speak in first-person narrative: "In the story I've lived, ..." not "People generally think..."

## Examples

**Topic:** "Failure is something to be ashamed of."

> **Dominant story identified:** Failure signals incompetence. Successful people don't fail — or if they do, they hide it. Admitting failure loses credibility.
>
> **Externalize — Devil's Advocate (ENTP):** Let's name this force. Call it "The Verdict." The Verdict tells people that a single failure defines their worth. How has The Verdict shaped how people in this room talk about their own mistakes?
>
> **Pragmatist (ESTJ):** I've never told a client about a project that went badly. The Verdict says that would end the relationship.
>
> **Unique outcome — Empathizer (INFP):** But I once told a client about a mistake before they noticed — and they trusted me more afterward, not less. That broke The Verdict's logic entirely for me.
>
> **Alternative story emerging — Analyst (INTP):** What if the alternative story is: transparency about failure is a signal of competence, not its absence? The unique outcomes suggest that story already exists — it's just not the loudest one.
