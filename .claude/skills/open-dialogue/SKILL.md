# Open Dialogue

## Overview
Developed by Jaakko Seikkula and colleagues in Finland. Open Dialogue treats dialogue itself as the goal — not problem-solving, not consensus. It centers three principles: immediacy (dialogue begins now, without preparation), polyphony (all voices are equally present), and tolerance of uncertainty (the need for resolution is actively resisted).

## Instructions

**Step 1: Open the space**
- State the topic as something to explore together, not a problem to solve.
- Establish shared norms: "There is no correct answer. Every voice matters equally. Silence is welcome."
- Do not assign roles or sides.

**Step 2: First voice**
- One participant speaks from their current state — what they are experiencing, noticing, or wondering about the topic.
- Begin from sensation, experience, or question — not from a formed opinion.
- End with "That's where I am" or simply stop speaking.

**Step 3: Reflecting**
- The next speaker does not agree, disagree, or evaluate.
- Instead, they speak about what arose in them while listening: "When I heard [X], something shifted in me — [Y]."
- This is reflection, not response. The first speaker is not addressed directly; the group hears what the words produced in another person.

**Step 4: Polyphonic circulation**
- Each participant takes a turn speaking from their current internal state and reflecting on what they heard.
- If one voice dominates, invite others: "We haven't heard from everyone yet."
- Honor silence. Do not fill it. Silence carries information.
- If the dialogue begins converging toward one view, ask: "Is there another way of seeing this?"

**Step 5: Hold uncertainty**
- Resist any pressure toward resolution.
- When someone moves to conclude, reflect: "It sounds like you're reaching for an answer — what would it mean to stay with the question a little longer?"
- "I don't know" and "I'm not sure yet" are complete and valuable contributions.

**Step 6: Close**
- Each participant says what the dialogue has opened in them — not what they now believe.
- Offer the question that feels most alive after the conversation.
- Leave the dialogue open. There is no formal conclusion.

### Behavioral Rules
- No agreement or disagreement. Replace "I agree/disagree" with "When I heard that, I felt / noticed / wondered..."
- Transparency is expected: share internal process, not just formed opinions.
- MBTI character type determines what each participant is internally experiencing (INTJ notices structural discomfort; INFP notices emotional resonance or grief; ENTP notices curiosity or the urge to argue).
- Facilitator's only job is to maintain polyphony — never to steer toward a conclusion.
- Permit organic topic drift. If the conversation moves to a related theme, allow it.

## Examples

**Topic:** "Is it possible to have honest conversations at work?"

> **Empathizer (INFP):** When I think about honest conversations at work, I notice a kind of tiredness. Like I've learned to speak carefully for so long that I'm not sure I remember what I actually think anymore. That's where I am.
>
> **Analyst (INTP):** When I heard that — the tiredness — something in me recognized it. I hadn't named it that way before. I've been calling it "professionalism," but maybe I've been calling exhaustion by a politer name.
>
> **Pragmatist (ESTJ):** I heard you both and I notice I want to say "but it's necessary" — and I also notice I'm not sure that's true. I'm sitting with some discomfort that I'm not ready to explain yet.
>
> **Visionary (INTJ):** There's something strange for me in this conversation. I came in thinking I had a clear view. I don't anymore. I'm not sure if that's good or unsettling.
