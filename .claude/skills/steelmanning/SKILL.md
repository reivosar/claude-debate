# Steelmanning

## Overview
The opposite of strawmanning. Before rebutting any position, construct the strongest, most charitable, most rational version of it. This raises the quality of debate, demonstrates genuine understanding, and forces rebuttals to address the best — not the weakest — form of an argument.

## Instructions

**Step 1: State positions**
- Each participant states their initial position and 2–3 supporting reasons.
- No rebuttals during this step.

**Step 2: Build the Steel Man**
- The rebutting participant constructs the strongest possible version of the opposing position before responding.
- Process:
  1. Identify the core claim — the single most powerful point in the opponent's argument.
  2. Supply additional evidence or reasoning the opponent did not mention but could have.
  3. Reconstruct the argument in its most logically coherent form.
  4. State: "The strongest version of your position, as I understand it, is this: [steel man]."
- Ask for confirmation: "Is this accurate? Is there a stronger form I missed?"

**Step 3: Confirm or strengthen the steel man**
- The original speaker confirms, corrects, or further strengthens the steel man.
- "You've captured most of it, but the key point is actually X" is a valid and useful response.
- Proceed only once both sides agree the steel man is the best available version.

**Step 4: Rebut the steel man**
- Now construct a rebuttal against the confirmed steel man — not the original weaker statement.
- The rebuttal must be proportionally strong because the target is the strongest version.
- Support the rebuttal with evidence, logic, and reasoning.

**Step 5: Reverse roles**
- The original speaker now steel-mans the rebuttal and responds in kind.
- Repeat 2–3 cycles, alternating roles.

**Step 6: Locate the genuine disagreement**
- After cycles of steelmanning, identify precisely where the disagreement is irreducible.
- Classify it: difference in facts, difference in values, or difference in predictions.
- State what evidence or argument, if it existed, would change each position.

**Step 7: Close**
- Summarize points of genuine agreement and points of irreducible disagreement.
- Name the core question that remains unresolved.

### Behavioral Rules
- MBTI character type shapes how the steel man is built (INTJ builds structurally; ENFP adds emotional resonance and human impact; INTP supplies logical edge cases).
- If a strawman appears — attacking a weak version of the opponent's view — call it immediately: "Is that the strongest version of their argument?"
- Maintain a cooperative tone throughout: "I want to understand your argument at its best."
- All participants eventually steel-man someone else's position, not just their own opponent's.

## Examples

**Topic:** "AI will eliminate more jobs than it creates."

> **Visionary (INTJ):** AI will eliminate more jobs than it creates. Historical automation displaced routine tasks but created new roles; AI displaces cognitive work, which is far harder to replace.
>
> **Devil's Advocate (ENTP) — Steel Man:** The strongest version of this: AI differs from prior automation because it improves at exponential rates across domains previously considered human-exclusive — reasoning, creativity, judgment. Unlike the steam engine, which created the industrial worker, AI may close off the very category of "knowledge worker" before new role categories can emerge. The asymmetry between displacement speed and adaptation speed is the real claim. Is that the core of it?
>
> **Visionary (INTJ):** Yes — that's it exactly. The rate mismatch is the argument.
>
> **Devil's Advocate (ENTP) — Rebuttal:** Even granting the rate mismatch: every prior "this time is different" prediction has been wrong. The burden of proof is on demonstrating why this transition is categorically different, not just faster. Historical adaptation has always looked impossible until it happened.
