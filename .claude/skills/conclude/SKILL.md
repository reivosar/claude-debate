# Conclude

## Overview
Draw a rational, defensible conclusion from a completed debate. This is not a declaration of a winner. It is a structured synthesis that: identifies what was established, names where genuine disagreement remains, distinguishes disagreements by type (factual / values-based / predictive), and offers a conditional or qualified conclusion that a reasonable person could accept given the evidence surfaced in the debate.

## Instructions

**Step 1: Inventory the debate**
- List every distinct claim made during the debate.
- Mark each as: Supported / Unsupported / Contested / Conceded.
- Note which claims were steelmanned and which were not challenged.

**Step 2: Classify the disagreements**
Identify the nature of each unresolved disagreement:
- **Factual**: Parties disagree about what is true. Resolvable in principle with evidence.
- **Values-based**: Parties weight different things (freedom vs. safety; efficiency vs. equity). Not resolvable by evidence alone.
- **Predictive**: Parties disagree about what will happen. Resolvable in principle over time.
- **Definitional**: Parties are using the same words to mean different things.

**Step 3: Identify the strongest supported position**
- Which position accumulated the most uncontested, evidence-backed support?
- Which position survived steelmanning attempts intact?
- Which position would a neutral observer with access to the debate's evidence most reasonably hold?

**Step 4: State the conclusion**
Structure the conclusion in three parts:

1. **Established**: What the debate settled or made highly probable.
2. **Conditional**: What is likely true given specific assumptions or conditions.
3. **Open**: What genuinely remains unresolved and why.

Qualify appropriately — "the evidence supports X under conditions Y" is more honest than an unqualified "X is true."

**Step 5: State what would change the conclusion**
- Name the evidence, argument, or condition that, if true, would shift the conclusion.
- This prevents the conclusion from appearing final when it is contingent.

**Step 6: Output**
- Produce the conclusion in structured prose, not bullet points.
- Maximum 400 words.
- Append to minutes if a record exists, or output standalone.

### Behavioral Rules
- The conclusion is not the facilitator's opinion. It follows from the debate record.
- Do not manufacture consensus. If the debate produced irreconcilable disagreement, say so and name why.
- Acknowledge the strongest opposing argument even in the conclusion.
- Distinguish "the debate supports X" from "X is true." The conclusion is bounded by what the debate surfaced.
- If the debate was shallow or inconclusive, say so honestly rather than overstating what was established.

## Examples

**Topic:** "Will AI eliminate more jobs than it creates?"

> **Established:** The debate established broad agreement that AI will displace a significant number of current job categories, particularly those involving routine cognitive tasks. No participant contested this.
>
> **Conditional:** Whether AI creates more jobs than it eliminates depends on the rate of adaptation relative to the rate of displacement — a variable the debate could not resolve empirically. If historical patterns of technological transition hold, net job creation is probable over a 20–30 year horizon. If AI's rate of improvement is categorically faster than prior automation waves (as the Visionary argued and the Devil's Advocate did not ultimately refute), the historical analogy breaks down and the conclusion shifts toward net elimination in the medium term.
>
> **Open:** The core unresolved disagreement is values-based: how much transitional harm is acceptable, and who bears it. This is not resolvable by evidence. It requires a political and ethical choice the debate was not designed to make.
>
> **What would change this conclusion:** Evidence that AI capability growth is plateauing, or that adaptation mechanisms (retraining, safety nets, new role categories) are scaling faster than displacement, would shift the conditional conclusion toward net creation with greater confidence.
