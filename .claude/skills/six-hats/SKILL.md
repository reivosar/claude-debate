# Six Thinking Hats

## Overview
Edward de Bono's parallel thinking method. Each of six colored hats represents a distinct mode of thinking. All participants wear the same hat simultaneously, focusing collective attention on one mode at a time. This replaces adversarial debate with coordinated exploration.

## Instructions

**Hat Reference**

| Hat | Mode | Focus |
|-----|------|-------|
| White | Objective / Facts | Data, known information, gaps in knowledge |
| Red | Emotion / Intuition | Feelings, gut reactions, no justification required |
| Black | Critical / Caution | Risks, problems, reasons it might fail |
| Yellow | Optimistic / Value | Benefits, value, reasons it might succeed |
| Green | Creative / Alternatives | New ideas, alternatives, possibilities |
| Blue | Process / Overview | Managing the thinking, summarizing, next steps |

**Step 1: Blue Hat — Open**
- Define the topic clearly.
- State the goal of this session.
- Announce the hat sequence to be used.

**Step 2: White Hat — Facts**
- All participants share known facts, data, and statistics relevant to the topic.
- No opinions or interpretations. State only "X is known" or "X is not yet known."
- Explicitly name what information is missing.

**Step 3: Red Hat — Emotions**
- All participants share their emotional reaction or gut feeling about the topic.
- No justification required. "I feel uneasy about this" is a complete statement.
- Do not evaluate or challenge emotional responses.

**Step 4: Black Hat — Caution**
- All participants identify risks, weaknesses, and reasons the idea might fail.
- Be specific. "It might not work" is insufficient; "It fails when X condition holds" is better.
- Pessimism is a feature here, not a flaw.

**Step 5: Yellow Hat — Optimism**
- All participants identify benefits, strengths, and reasons the idea might succeed.
- Ground optimism in reasoning: explain why the benefit is realistic.

**Step 6: Green Hat — Creativity**
- All participants propose new ideas, alternatives, and modifications.
- Feasibility is not a constraint in this hat. Build on others' ideas rather than rejecting them.

**Step 7: Blue Hat — Close**
- Summarize key outputs from each hat.
- State conclusions, open questions, and proposed next actions.

### Behavioral Rules
- Label each statement with its hat color: **[Black Hat] Pragmatist (ESTJ):**
- MBTI character type colors the thinking within a hat (e.g., ESTJ under Black Hat produces detailed risk checklists; ENFP under Green Hat generates rapid-fire possibilities).
- If a participant speaks out of hat, name it: "That's a Black Hat thought — save it for that round."
- The Blue Hat role may be held by one character throughout as a facilitator.

## Examples

**Topic:** "Should our organization adopt a four-day work week?"

> **[White Hat] Analyst (INTP):** A 2022 Iceland trial covering 2,500 workers showed productivity maintained or improved in 85% of participating organizations. We lack data on our own sector specifically.
>
> **[Red Hat] Advocate (ENFJ):** I feel genuine excitement about this — and also a quiet anxiety that it might create pressure to compress the same workload.
>
> **[Black Hat] Pragmatist (ESTJ):** Client-facing roles cannot simply drop a day. If we reduce availability, we risk service-level agreement breaches.
>
> **[Yellow Hat] Activist (ENFP):** Retention and recruitment advantages could be significant. In a tight labor market, this differentiates us from competitors.
>
> **[Green Hat] Devil's Advocate (ENTP):** What if we piloted it for one team only and measured against a control group internally?
